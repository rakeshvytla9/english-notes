## üß† Fundamental Concepts

### 1. The Definition
The verb in a sentence must agree with the **Subject** in terms of:
*   **Number:** Singular Subject $\rightarrow$ Singular Verb | Plural Subject $\rightarrow$ Plural Verb.
*   **Person:** 1st, 2nd, or 3rd Person.

### 2. The Golden Rule (S-V-O)
> **Concept:** The Verb changes *only* according to the Subject. The Object has *no influence* on the verb.

### 3. Identifying the "Real" Subject
To apply the rule correctly, you must find the actual subject. Be aware of these three patterns:

#### A. The Prepositional Trap
When two nouns are connected by a preposition, the noun *after* the preposition is an object. The verb agrees with the **first noun**.
```text
Structure: [Noun 1 (Main Subject)] + Preposition + [Noun 2 (Object)] + Verb
```
*   *Example:* The **quality** of apples **is** good. (Verb agrees with *Quality*, not *Apples*)

#### B. The Inversion Rule
In sentences starting with adverbs of place (like *Here, There, Along*), the subject is placed **after** the verb.
```text
Structure: Adverb + Verb + Subject
```
*   *Example:* Along the border **are** the **Himalayas**.
*   *Example:* There **were** many **boys** in the class.

#### C. Gerunds
If a sentence starts with `V1+ing` (Gerund) acting as a noun, it acts as a Singular Subject.
*   *Example:* **Smoking is** injurious.

## ‚ö° Types & Rules (Exam Specific)

### TYPE-01: The Conjunction 'AND'
**Rule:** Two different subjects joined by 'and' take a **Plural Verb**.
*   *Example:* Ramesh **and** Mahesh **have** qualified.

### TYPE-02: Pair Words (Single Idea)
**Rule:** If two nouns joined by 'and' represent a single unit, idea, or person, use a **Singular Verb**.
*   *Example:* **Slow and steady wins** the race.
*   *Example:* **Bread and butter is** essential for life.

### TYPE-03: The "More Than" Concept
This rule depends on the noun that immediately follows "More than".

| Structure | Verb Agreement | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **More than one** + Singular Noun | **Singular Verb** | More than **one student has** passed. |
| **More than two/three** + Plural Noun | **Plural Verb** | More than **two thieves have** been caught. |

### TYPE-04: Concept of "Many"
**Rule:** Used with Countable nouns. Indicates a large number.
```text
Many + Plural Noun + Plural Verb
```
*   *Example:* **Many officers are** involved.

### TYPE-05: Concept of "Many a / Many an"
**Rule:** Indicates many, but in a distributive sense (one by one).
```text
Many + a/an + Singular Noun + Singular Verb
```
*   *Example:* **Many a book has** been published.

### TYPE-06: Concept of "Much"
**Rule:** Used with Uncountable nouns or as a Pronoun. Always Singular.
```text
Much + Uncountable Noun + Singular Verb
OR
Much (as subject) + Singular Verb
```
*   *Example:* **Much milk has** been wasted.
*   *Example:* **Much has** happened since you left.
